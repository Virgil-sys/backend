History of Victoria Falls

Explore stories, legends, and milestones from Africa's most legendary waterfall and the cultures shaped by it.

The Main Falls

Known as Mosi-oa-Tunya (“The Smoke That Thunders”) by the local Kololo people, Victoria Falls is the largest curtain of falling water in the world, plunging over 100 meters into the Batoka Gorge and creating a constant mist and roar visible and heard for miles.

Livingstone Island

This tiny island on the edge of the falls is named after explorer David Livingstone, who was the first European to view the falls in 1855. He famously described them as “scenes so lovely must have been gazed upon by angels in their flight”.

Devil's Pool

A naturally formed pool perched on the very edge of the falls, Devil’s Pool is accessible during low water season (usually August to January) and offers fearless visitors a chance to swim at nature’s edge under the careful guidance of local guides.

Victoria Falls Bridge

Completed in 1905, the bridge was a feat of Edwardian engineering, spanning 198 meters between Zimbabwe and Zambia. It was originally part of Cecil Rhodes’ vision for a Cape-to-Cairo railway, and today is an iconic site for bungee jumping and gorge swings.

Local Culture

For centuries, the Tonga, Lozi, Shona, and Ndebele peoples have called this region home—crafting legends, songs, and traditions around the Falls. The area remains rich in craftwork, music, dance, and traditional ceremonies linked to the water and land.

Wildlife and the Rainforest

Thanks to the permanent spray from the falls, a unique rainforest ecosystem thrives—sheltering rare plants, birds, and wildlife. From baboons and bushbucks to colorful kingfishers and the occasional elephant, the area remains a sanctuary for many species.

Kololo People's Legends

Long before explorers, the Kololo and other tribes revered the falls as sacred. They believed Mosi-oa-Tunya was home to powerful spirits—local ceremonies were (and are!) held to honor the river god Nyaminyami, protector of the valley and bringer of fortune or flood.

Colonial Encounters & Naming

The arrival of David Livingstone in 1855 marked a turning point. Naming the falls after Queen Victoria, Livingstone’s reports brought worldwide fame and inspired explorers, hunters, and traders to the region—changing local life and economies forever.

Early Tourism

By the early 1900s, steam trains brought travelers to marvel at the falls’ grandeur. Colonial hotels, lush gardens, and guided rainforest walks became staples—solidifying Victoria Falls as a premier adventure and luxury travel destination.

Conservation and UNESCO Status

Declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1989, Victoria Falls remains under joint protection by Zimbabwe and Zambia. Ongoing conservation ensures that its beauty, ecosystems, and cultural traditions remain unspoiled for future generations.
